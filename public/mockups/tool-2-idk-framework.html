Lesson 6.3 — Reference Tool
The "I Don't Know" Framework
The three words that build more credibility than any pitch deck ever will.
The Golden Rule
"I don't know" is not a weakness. It's a pivot point. What you do in the next 8 seconds determines whether you lose the deal or earn the trust.
The 3-Step Pivot
1
Acknowledge
Say it plainly. No hedging, no filler. The prospect already knows you don't know — the question is whether you'll admit it.
"That's a great question, and I want to give you the right answer — not a guess."
2
Bridge
Redirect to what you do know. Anchor the conversation in your competence while buying time for the gap.
"What I can tell you is how three similar companies handled this — and I'll get the specifics from our solutions team by end of day."
3
Commit
Give a specific timeframe. "I'll find out" is noise. "I'll have the answer by 4pm today" is a commitment.
"I'll confirm the exact integration timeline and send it to you by 4pm today. Fair?"
Ready-Made Scripts
When asked about pricing you can't quote
"Pricing depends on a few factors I want to understand better. Can I ask two questions so I can get you an accurate range by tomorrow?"
When asked about a feature you're unsure of
"I don't want to give you a maybe — let me check with our product team and confirm by end of day. In the meantime, here's what I know works..."
When asked about a competitor
"I'd rather give you an honest comparison than a sales pitch. Let me pull the latest data and walk you through it on our next call."
When challenged on something technical
"That's outside my depth — I'll loop in our SE who can speak to the architecture. Can I set up a 15-minute call this week?"
Red Flags — What Not to Do
  • Guessing and hoping they won't notice
  • Saying "I think so" when you have no idea
  • Changing the subject without acknowledging the gap
  • Over-promising a timeline you can't keep
  • Apologizing excessively for not knowing
  • Blaming someone else ("Marketing didn't tell us")
