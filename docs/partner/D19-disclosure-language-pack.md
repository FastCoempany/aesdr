# D19 — Disclosure Language Pack

**Deliverable:** Approved disclosure phrasings for every channel a partner or host may use. Required reading + required compliance. The host follows the same rules.
**Audience:** Partners (operators, coaches, creators, alumni); the AESDR host; founder.
**Voice:** 50/50 — clarity over voice. Disclosure is plain by design.
**Format:** Markdown source; ships as a one-pager PDF inside the partner enablement kit. Also embedded in partner-rider language and host contract.
**Canon refs:** `AFFILIATE_BRAND_CANON.md` §10.1 (FTC), §10.4 (CAN-SPAM), §10.5 (TCPA), §10.6 (host).

> **Rule of thumb.** Disclose any time AESDR has paid, comped, gifted, traded, or otherwise materially connected with the person making the recommendation. Place the disclosure clearly, conspicuously, and *before or alongside* the recommendation — never buried, never abbreviated past recognition, never in language a reasonable reader would miss.

## A. Channel-by-channel approved phrasings

### A1. Newsletter / blog post

Use one of these inline, near the link, not in the footer.

> *"Affiliate disclosure: I earn a commission if you enroll through this link."*

> *"Heads up — this is an affiliate link, which means I'm paid a commission when someone enrolls. AESDR is a course I'd be sharing either way."*

**Don't:** bury in the footer, abbreviate to "aff link," or rely on a generic site-wide disclosure page.

### A2. Twitter / X post

End the tweet with `#ad` or `#sponsored`. Plain text. No sneaky placement.

> *"AESDR's first 1–2 year SaaS sales course is the best operating-manual format I've seen for SDRs / AEs. Workshop pilot for [community] members on [date]: [link] #ad"*

If the post contains a quote-thread or media, the disclosure goes in the **first** post, not the last.

### A3. Instagram / TikTok post (static or carousel)

Disclosure in the **first sentence** of the caption, before the recommendation.

> *"#ad — AESDR partner. The 12-lesson SaaS sales survival course built for first-1-to-2-year SDRs and AEs. Workshop into [community] on [date]: [link]"*

In-image disclosure is required if the caption is collapsed by default — overlay `Paid partnership with AESDR` in legible type.

### A4. Instagram Story / Reel / TikTok video

- **In-frame text:** `Paid partnership with AESDR` or `#ad` — visible for the duration of the recommendation, large enough to read on mobile.
- **Verbal disclosure:** within the first 10 seconds, in plain language: `"Quick disclosure — AESDR's a partner of mine. They're paying me a commission when someone enrolls through my link. Here's why I think the program's worth a look anyway…"`
- **Native paid-partnership tag:** use the platform's native disclosure tool *in addition to* the verbal/text disclosure, never as a substitute.

### A5. Live workshop (when partner is co-hosting or introducing)

At the partner intro, on-screen and verbal:

> *"Quick disclosure before [Host name] takes over: AESDR is a partner of [community/program]. We're paid a commission when our members enroll through this workshop. We'd only be running this session if I thought it was worth your time — but you should know the arrangement."*

The workshop registration page also carries the disclosure (see A8).

### A6. YouTube / podcast

- **Pinned-comment disclosure** on YouTube; **show-notes disclosure** on podcast.
- **Verbal disclosure** in the first 60 seconds: `"This episode includes a paid partnership with AESDR. Disclosure aside, here's why we wanted to talk about it…"`
- If AESDR is mentioned multiple times across an episode, one verbal disclosure at the top is sufficient under FTC; a repeat at the explicit recommendation is best practice.

### A7. Affiliate link in DM (Slack, Discord, Instagram DM, email reply, text)

Plain one-line disclosure in the same message as the link:

> *"Heads up — I get a commission if you enroll. Link: [partner-tracked URL]"*

Never send a tracked link without disclosure. Even in a 1:1 DM. Especially in a 1:1 DM — the FTC has been explicit that material connection disclosures apply to private channels too.

### A8. Workshop registration page (AESDR-built; partner-co-branded)

Below the registration form, in serif body, muted color:

> *"Workshop hosted by AESDR in partnership with [community]. [Community] receives a commission on enrollments that come through this page. AESDR sets the curriculum, content, and pricing; [community] vouches for fit."*

This is canon copy — already on every partner-promo page (canon §8.1, §8.3).

### A9. Email — AESDR-sent, mentioning a partner

Footer block, mono, muted:

> *"This message is from AESDR ([postal address]). You're receiving it because you registered for [Workshop / community partner]. Unsubscribe anytime: [unsubscribe link]."*

### A10. Email — partner-sent, mentioning AESDR

Inline disclosure in the body, not the footer:

> *"Quick disclosure: AESDR is a partner of [community/program]. We earn a commission if you enroll through the link below. We'd only recommend it if we thought it fit."*

Plus standard CAN-SPAM footer (sender postal address, unsubscribe).

### A11. SMS — AESDR-sent (with consent on file)

160-character ceiling. Disclosure handled at consent capture (the registration form's separate SMS opt-in checkbox), not in every message. Each marketing SMS includes:

> *"AESDR: [content]. Reply STOP to opt out."*

If a partner wants to text *their own* list about AESDR, the partner is the seller of record on their own consent. AESDR is not a piggyback recipient under FCC one-to-one consent rules. (See canon §10.5.)

## B. Mistakes that look fine but aren't

| What partners try | Why it fails |
|---|---|
| `#aff` or `#affiliatelink` alone | FTC has indicated abbreviations like these are unclear to a reasonable consumer. Use `#ad` or `#sponsored` or the full word. |
| Site-wide disclosure page only | Disclosure must be *near* the recommendation, not at the foot of the site. |
| Disclosure in a hashtag block at the bottom of a long IG caption | If the caption collapses, the disclosure is hidden. Top of caption, or in-image. |
| "I love AESDR!" with disclosure in a different post | Each recommendation needs its own disclosure. |
| Verbal disclosure muttered in 0.4 seconds before fast cut | The standard is "clear and conspicuous." Speed-talking is not conspicuous. |
| "Use my code" without saying you get paid | The code is an inducement; the connection still has to be disclosed. |

## C. Visual treatment of in-image / on-screen disclosures

For Stories, Reels, video overlays:

- **Type:** Barlow Condensed (`var(--cond)`), 700wt, uppercase, .03em letter-spacing.
- **Size:** minimum 18pt at 1080×1920 (Reels/Stories), 24pt at 1920×1080 (YouTube).
- **Color:** pure white on dark backgrounds; `var(--ink)` on light. No grey-on-grey low-contrast disclosure. *Conspicuous* is the legal standard.
- **Duration:** visible for the entire duration of the recommendation, not just a flash.
- **Placement:** top-third of the frame, never inside platform UI areas (Stories caption bar, TikTok progress bar) where it gets cropped.

A mock-up sheet is part of the design queue (Tier 2 per the deliverable plan); for now this canonical text governs.

## D. Approval gate

Per canon §16, every paid placement and ephemeral content piece requires AESDR pre-approval before publication. Disclosure is part of every review. If a partner ships without pre-approval *and* without proper disclosure, that's two strikes — pilot terminates immediately.

**Upstream gate:** Disclosure compliance is one of the canonical checks in D27 partner vetting scorecard (§3.3 + §4 + §5.3). A partner who declines disclosure language during D27 vetting fails the scorecard outright — D27 §"Red-flag list" — and never reaches the point of pre-approval-per-asset under §16. This document governs *post-PASS* execution; D27 is the pre-PASS gate.

---

## E. The "good operator" stance

We frame disclosure as a feature of working with us, not a tax. The partner can say:

> *"AESDR is one of the only sales courses I'd actively partner on. They make us follow real disclosure rules, which is honestly why I trust the program more, not less."*

That stance turns compliance into a positioning move. If a partner can't endorse that frame, the fit isn't there.
