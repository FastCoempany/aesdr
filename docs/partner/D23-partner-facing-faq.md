## Q02 — How much time does the program take?

> *12 lessons. About 45–60 minutes per lesson, including the worksheet and the takeaway tool. Across 6–8 weeks at one to two lessons per week is the design pace; some students go faster, some slower.*
>
> *The Discord (Untamed) is asynchronous — you can dip in for 5 minutes a week or 5 minutes a day. Most active members read more than they post.*
>
> *The honest answer: if you have an hour a week and the will to actually do the worksheet, you'll finish. If you don't have the hour, the worksheet is what you'll skip first, and the worksheet is where the work is.*

**Notes (internal):**
- Per canon §13, the "honest answer" beat is the move. Prospects know they overcommit; saying so earns trust.
- "The worksheet is where the work is" is candidate canonical (carries Rowan's verdict structure).

## Q03 — What's the format?

> *Each lesson has four pieces:*
>
> *— A 30–45 minute video lesson, hosted by [HOST_FIRST_NAME].*
> *— A worksheet you fill out against your real reps, your real pipeline, your real comp plan. Not "imagine a prospect named Bob."*
> *— A takeaway tool — a one-page operating sheet you can use the next day. (Twelve lessons, twelve takeaways. Twelve things you can put on your desk.)*
> *— A discussion thread in Untamed, our Discord. Other students working on the same lesson are in there.*
>
> *No live cohort. No mandatory office hours. No drip-gating — every lesson is open from day one and you decide the pace.*

**Notes (internal):**
- "Untamed" is canonical (canon §15 glossary).
- "Twelve lessons, twelve takeaways. Twelve things you can put on your desk." is verdict-shaped Rowan and tracks against the §14 tagline *"12 lessons. 5 tools. 1 new you."* The "5 tools" delta is intentional — the tagline counts the major tools, not every lesson takeaway.
- "Drip-gating" is the SaaS-norm pattern we're countering. Naming it is the move.

## Q04 — What happens with the workshop replay?

> *The replay opens within 10 minutes of the live workshop ending. It's available for 72 hours. After that, the link expires and we don't run "evergreen" replays.*
>
> *Why the cap: we run live workshops for the conversation. The replay is a courtesy for people who couldn't make the live, not a content asset we're trying to scale. We'd rather host the next live than re-host last week's recording.*

**Notes (internal):**
- Anti-fake-urgency stance (canon §9.2) — the 72-hour expiry is real and explained, not theatricalized.
- The "we'd rather host the next live" answer separates AESDR from evergreen-funnel SaaS courses. Canon §1.1 workshop-first.

## Q05 — How is this different from free content on YouTube / LinkedIn / podcasts?

> *You're right that there is a near-infinite supply of free content about SaaS sales. And yet, you've been in the role for months or years and the gap is still there. Either the free content isn't the answer, or it is and you haven't found the right thread yet. We think it's the first one, and here's why.*
>
> ***Three things free content cannot do:***
>
> ***Sequence.*** *Free content is published in the order the publisher feels like publishing. You're left to assemble it into a curriculum yourself, with no way to know whether you're reading Lesson 1 or Lesson 8 or a hot take that contradicts both. AESDR is sequenced. You move from foundational to advanced in a deliberate order, and the order is the work.*
>
> ***Frame.*** *Free content optimizes for engagement, which means it rewards confidence, controversy, and short hooks. It tells you "always be closing" and never tells you what to actually say. AESDR is the operating manual — the part the free content skipped because it doesn't perform well in a 60-second clip.*
>
> ***Accountability.*** *Free content is consumed alone, nodded at, and forgotten. AESDR has interactive worksheets, takeaway tools, and a Discord (Untamed) where reps in your situation are talking about what's working. Doing the work, watched by other people doing the work, is the difference between watching and ramping.*
>
> *None of this is to say you should stop watching free content. Watch it. Learn from it. AESDR isn't a replacement for free content; it's the structure you can hang the free content on once you have one.*

**Notes (internal):**
- This is the canonical lift from D15 (objection email). The argument is the same; the surface is different. **Do not improve.** Pattern-stability earns trust.
- Per canon §3.3, this is verdict mode (80/20). Michael does not appear in this answer — the argument earns its weight by being plain.
- If a partner audience has a strong reaction to this answer in the registration-page A/B, that's a fit signal worth logging in D33 postmortem.

>
> *Two practical paths:*
>
> *— **Self-purchase, then reimburse.** Most companies will reimburse a $249–$299 sales-training program against an itemized invoice. We provide the invoice on enrollment.*

>
> *Honest note: if your manager tells you "we already pay for [some other program] — use that," they may be right. AESDR is not always the answer. We'd rather you use the right resource than buy ours under pressure.*

## Q07 — Who should not buy AESDR?

> *— If you're 8+ years in sales and aren't open to a re-look at fundamentals, the first five lessons will bore you.*
> *— If you've enrolled in three programs in the last year and finished none of them, the issue isn't free vs paid; it's whether you'll do the work this time. AESDR is structured to finish, but we can't structure your hour-a-week for you.*
> *— If you want motivational content, this isn't that. Save your money for content that's structured to entertain.*
> *— If you're not in a sales role yet — if you're trying to break in — AESDR isn't a "how to get into sales" course. We assume you have the seat.*
> *— If your primary problem is your manager, your team, or your comp plan, AESDR helps you read those conditions but won't fix them. Some operating problems require operating change.*
>
> *None of these are insults. They're filters. The 14-day refund makes the cost of trying low; the cost of buying-when-uncertain is your attention. Save it for something you're sure of.*

**Notes (internal):**
- Per canon §13, the honest disqualification is one of the brand's signature moves. This is the canonical version — every other surface that does disqualification (D09 slide 17, D15 email, D18 deadline email) lifts from here or quotes it.
- Per canon §3.3, this answer carries 30% Michael (slightly higher than the 20% verdict-mode default) — the named specifics ("three programs in the last year") are Michael's register.
- Pattern-stability discipline: do not edit this list lightly. Updates require a canon-revision PR (canon §17).

## Q08 — Do you give a certificate or credential?

> *No.*
>
> *We don't issue a credential because no SaaS sales credential currently has hiring weight in the market we serve, and we're not going to manufacture one and pretend it does. The thing that has hiring weight is the rep you become — measurable in the conversations you can hold, the pipeline you can read, the meetings you can run.*
>
> *If you need a line on your LinkedIn, "AESDR · 12-lesson operating curriculum" is honest, accurate, and can go under Education or Certifications. We won't object. But we won't issue a PDF that pretends to be more than that.*

**Notes (internal):**
- Per canon §1.6, honesty is the differentiator. Most competitor courses sell the certificate as if it carries weight. We name the truth.
- Per canon §4.1 banned vocab, "credential" is fine; "badge" is not. The phrasing here is intentional.
- The "we won't object" line is the cleanest version of letting the buyer use the thing they bought without us pretending we built more than we did.

## Q09 — Why now? Why not wait until I'm promoted to AE?

> *Two reasons.*
>
> *One: the gap between SDR and AE is mostly about judgment. The reps who get promoted are the ones who already operate like AEs before the title arrives — they read pipeline cleanly, they handle objections without escalating, they manage their manager. Waiting until the title arrives to build those muscles means the title arrives, and then your AE ramp starts at zero. Reps who built the muscles earlier ramp faster.*
>
> *Two: the SDR seat itself is more survivable when you have judgment. Most reps don't get fired from SDR for missing pipeline; they get fired for not knowing why they missed it. AESDR is the lens for "why."*
>
> *Honest counter: if you're 60+ days from promotion and your current quota is fine, waiting is also a defensible answer. The course will run again. The cohort cost of waiting is your time, not your seat.*

**Notes (internal):**
- The "why now" question is one of the two highest-leverage objections in the partner kit (the other is "free vs structured"). Both have full-paragraph answers because both deserve them.
- Per canon §13, the "honest counter" beat names the case where waiting is right. That move is the brand.
- "Cohort cost of waiting is your time, not your seat" — candidate canonical phrase.

## Q11 — How does the partner relationship work?

> *AESDR runs pilot partnerships with a small number of community operators, bootcamp coaches, and creators whose audiences match the early-career SaaS-sales role we serve. When you registered through one of those partners' links, two things became true:*
>
> *— The partner receives a commission on enrollments through their link, per FTC disclosure rules. The commission does not change the price you pay or the program you receive.*
> *— The workshop you attended was the same workshop every other pilot audience attends. Same deck, same offer, same pricing for this pilot. The partner doesn't customize the content — they bring the audience; we bring the program.*
>
> *We disclose this because honest disclosure is the brand. If a partner promotes AESDR without disclosing the commission, we ask them to add the disclosure, and if they refuse, we end the pilot.*

**Notes (internal):**
- Per canon §10.1, this is the FTC material-connection disclosure done in plain English. Required surface on the FAQ.
- Per canon §1.6, naming the partner relationship honestly is the differentiator from courses that pretend partner endorsements are unpaid.
- The "if they refuse, we end the pilot" line is canonical doctrine (canon §1.4, §1.6) and should not be paraphrased.

>
