## Section 1: The First 72 Hours at a New SDR Job

**What nobody tells you on Day 1.**

Your first week sets the tone for your entire tenure. Most SDRs walk in, get a Salesforce login, shadow someone for two days, and then get thrown into the deep end. Here's what to actually do.

**The 72-Hour Checklist:**

1. **Map the power structure.** Who is your direct manager? Who is their manager? Who controls quota changes? Who approves comp plan exceptions? Write these names down on Day 1. You'll need them.

2. **Find the top performer and buy them coffee.** Not your manager — the actual top-performing SDR. Ask them one question: "What do you do that nobody told you to do?" Their answer is worth more than your entire onboarding deck.

3. **Audit the CRM before you touch it.** Open Salesforce. Look at 20 closed-won deals from the last 6 months. How many touches did they take? What channels? What was the average deal cycle? This tells you what actually works here — not what your manager thinks works.

4. **Set your own baseline.** Before anyone gives you a quota, decide: what does a "good week" look like for YOU? Not the official number. Your number. Because when your manager says you're behind, you need your own scoreboard.

5. **Protect your mornings.** Block 8-10am for prospecting on Day 1. Don't wait until week 3 when your calendar is full of "team syncs" and "culture chats." Your mornings belong to outbound. Guard them.

**The meta-lesson:** Nobody will teach you this. The company's onboarding is designed to make you compliant, not effective. Your job is to be effective.

## Section 2: The 5 Manager Types (and How to Survive Each One)

Every SDR manager falls into one of five archetypes. Knowing which one you have changes everything about how you communicate, push back, and protect your career.

### Type 1: The Metric Maniac
**How to spot them:** They open every 1:1 with your activity numbers. Calls, emails, connects. They live in dashboards.

**How to survive:** Don't fight the metrics — reframe them. Instead of "I only made 80 calls," say "I made 80 calls, connected with 12, and 3 are in active sequences with engagement." Give them numbers, but wrap the numbers in a story.

### Type 2: The Template Tyrant
**How to spot them:** They mandate scripts, email templates, and talk tracks. Deviation is not tolerated.

**How to survive:** Follow their templates. But quietly A/B test your own versions. Track the data. When you have 30+ sends on each version, present the results: "I tested a variation and got 22% higher reply rate. Can I keep running it?" Data beats opinion.

### Type 3: The Ghost
**How to spot them:** You can't. They're never available. Your 1:1s get cancelled. Feedback is nonexistent.

**How to survive:** Create your own feedback loop. Send a weekly email every Friday: "Here's what I did, here's what worked, here's where I'm stuck, here's my plan for next week." Even if they never respond, you've created a paper trail that protects you in reviews.

### Type 4: The Cheerleader
**How to spot them:** Everything is "amazing" and "you're doing great" — even when you're not. They avoid hard conversations.

**How to survive:** Don't mistake praise for progress. Ask pointed questions: "What's one specific thing I should improve this week?" Force them to give you real feedback. If they can't, find a peer or skip-level who will.

### Type 5: The Builder
**How to spot them:** They actually invest in your development. They role-play, review your calls, and push back constructively. Rare.

**How to survive:** You don't survive this one — you thrive. Absorb everything. Take notes in every 1:1. This manager is your accelerator. Don't waste the opportunity by coasting.

## Section 3: The Cold Call Framework That Actually Works

Forget scripts. You need a structure.

**The 4-Block Cold Call:**

**Block 1: The Pattern Interrupt (0-8 seconds)**
Don't say "Hi, this is [name] from [company]." Everyone does that. Instead:

- "Hey [name], we haven't spoken before — do you have 27 seconds?"
- "[Name], I know this is out of the blue. Quick question before you hang up."
- "Hey, I'm calling about [specific thing happening at their company]. Is this a terrible time?"

The goal isn't to sound polished. It's to sound human enough that they don't hang up.

**Block 2: The Relevance Statement (8-20 seconds)**
Connect your call to something real. Not "we help companies like yours." Instead:

- "I saw your team just posted 3 SDR roles — when teams scale that fast, [specific problem] usually shows up."
- "I noticed you're running [specific tool]. Most teams at your stage hit [specific wall] around month 6."

**Block 3: The Question (20-35 seconds)**
One question. Not a pitch. A question that makes them think.

- "Is that something you're seeing, or am I off base?"
- "How are you handling that right now?"

**Block 4: The Next Step (35-60 seconds)**
If they engage, lock the next step. Not "I'll send you some info." Instead:

- "Can I get 15 minutes on your calendar this week to show you how [specific outcome]?"
- "Would Tuesday or Wednesday work for a quick look?"

If they say no, thank them and move on. No begging.

## Section 4: The Weekly Alignment Meeting (Template)

This is the single highest-leverage 30 minutes of your week. Most SDRs waste their 1:1 by letting their manager run it. Take control.

**Your 1:1 Agenda (send this to your manager 2 hours before the meeting):**

```
WEEKLY CHECK-IN — [Your Name] — [Date]

1. METRICS THIS WEEK
   - Calls: [X] | Connects: [X] | Meetings booked: [X]
   - Pipeline added: $[X]
   - Key conversion: [X]% connect-to-meeting

2. WINS
   - [1 specific win with context]

3. BLOCKERS
   - [1 specific blocker with proposed solution]

4. ASK
   - "What's one thing I should change this week?"

5. MY PLAN FOR NEXT WEEK
   - [2-3 bullet points]
```

**Why this works:** It flips the power dynamic. Your manager shows up to YOUR agenda. You control the narrative. You look proactive. And you have a weekly paper trail of your performance.

## Section 5: The Commission Math Nobody Does

Most SDRs don't understand their own comp plan well enough to reverse-engineer their behavior. Here's how.

**Step 1: Know your numbers.**
- Base salary: $[X]
- OTE: $[X]
- Variable (OTE minus base): $[X]
- Quota: $[X] pipeline or [X] meetings
- Commission per unit: $[X] per meeting / per qualified opp

**Step 2: Calculate your hourly rate at quota.**
- Working hours per month: ~168 (21 days × 8 hours)
- At 100% quota: OTE / 12 / 168 = your hourly rate
- Below quota: base / 12 / 168 = your floor rate

**Step 3: Calculate the value of each activity.**
- If you book 1 meeting per 50 calls, and each meeting is worth $[X] in commission...
- Then each call is worth $[X/50] in expected value
- Each connect is worth $[X/connects-per-meeting]

**Step 4: Find your leverage point.**
Look at your funnel. Where's the biggest drop-off?
- Calls → Connects: Is your connect rate below 5%? Work on timing and lists.
- Connects → Meetings: Below 15%? Work on your pitch.
- Meetings → Qualified: Below 60%? Work on your qualifying questions.

The highest-leverage improvement is always at the widest gap in your funnel. Fix that one thing and your commission follows.
