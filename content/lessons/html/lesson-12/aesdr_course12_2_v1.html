Section 01 — The Disconnect
Presence vs. Proximity

As an AE working from home, you're technically "there" all day. But between back-to-back Zoom demos, deal strategy sessions, and CRM updates, your partner sees you in the house without ever actually having you present. The illusion of availability creates more resentment than the office ever did.

  • 01The Illusion: You're home for lunch, sitting at the kitchen table, theoretically available. But your mind is on the deal you just pitched and the follow-up you need to send.
  • 02The Reality: Your partner sees you physically present but mentally gone. Every notification pulls you back to pipeline. Every "one more email" steals another five minutes of connection.
  • 03The Cost: Your spouse starts to resent the laptop more than they'd resent a commute. At least with an office, there was a clear boundary. Now there's none.
The Reality Check
"Working from home should make life easier, right?"
It should—but the AE's always-on culture erases the boundary between deal mode and home mode. You're physically there, emotionally absent, and your partner feels it every day.

As an SDR working from home, you're in the house all day making calls, sending sequences, and grinding through your activity numbers. Your partner sees you sitting right there—but you might as well be in another zip code. The illusion of availability creates more friction than the office ever did.

  • 01The Illusion: You're home all day. Your partner assumes that means more time together. But your headset is on from 8 AM to 5 PM and you're mentally replaying objections at dinner.
  • 02The Reality: Between call blocks, CRM logging, and Slack messages from your manager, there's no actual break. Your partner gets the exhausted, depleted version of you.
  • 03The Cost: Your partner starts to resent the headset, the "hold on one sec" interruptions, and the fact that you're always half-listening. Proximity without presence is worse than distance.
The Reality Check
"Working from home should make life easier, right?"
It should—but the SDR grind doesn't stop just because you're in sweatpants. The activity pressure follows you from the desk to the couch. Your partner feels invisible.

Section 02 & 03 — The Burnout Trap
The Emotional Black Hole & Digital Overload

As an AE, your job demands massive emotional output. Every demo requires energy. Every negotiation requires focus. Every lost deal takes something out of you. By the time you close the laptop, you've given your best self to prospects—and your partner gets whatever's left.

  • 01The Leftovers: You spent an hour on a high-stakes demo, handled a tense negotiation, and dealt with a churning account. Your partner asks about dinner and you can barely form a sentence. They get the depleted version of you.
  • 02The Perception: To them, it feels like the deal always wins. Like the prospect's needs matter more than theirs. Like your pipeline is your real relationship.
  • 03The Digital Thief: After hours, you're still checking if the prospect opened your proposal. Slack pings from your SE. A LinkedIn message from a champion. Every notification steals another moment from your partner.
The Bottom Line
"When you're always half-plugged into the deal, your partner feels like they're losing."
Even if the deal is worth six figures, your relationship is worth more. The constant pings, the "let me just check this one thing," and the mental replay of negotiations erode the connection you need at home.

As an SDR, your job is an emotional grinder. Rejection after rejection, objection after objection, and the constant pressure to hit activity numbers. By the time you pull off the headset, you've spent your entire emotional tank on strangers who hung up on you—and your partner gets the fumes.

  • 01The Leftovers: You made 60 calls, got rejected 50 times, and booked 2 meetings. You should be proud, but you're just drained. Your partner asks how your day was and you can barely engage. They get the empty version of you.
  • 02The Perception: To them, it feels like your job matters more than they do. Like your manager's approval is more important than theirs. Like those calls drain you more than they should.
  • 03The Digital Thief: After hours, you're checking if your sequence emails got replies. Slack messages from your pod. Your manager asking about tomorrow's targets. Every notification steals another moment from your partner.
The Bottom Line
"When you're always replaying calls and checking metrics, your partner feels invisible."
Even if you're grinding toward promotion, your relationship needs investment too. The constant scrolling, the mental replays, and the "sorry, one more thing" erode the connection your partner needs.

Section 04 — Homework
Define Your Work-Life Rules
Task 1: Write Your Shutdown Ritual
  • What's one action you'll take to signal the end of your workday?
  • Examples: closing the laptop after sending your last follow-up, leaving the home office, changing out of "deal mode" clothes. Make it physical and visible so your partner sees the transition.
  • Examples: taking off the headset, closing the laptop, walking around the block. Make it physical and visible so your partner sees the transition from call mode to present mode.
Task 2: Set Non-Negotiable Quality Time
  • Identify two blocks of time each week where you'll be 100% present with your spouse.
  • Treat it with the same level of importance as your biggest client meeting. Put it on your calendar. Protect it like pipeline.
  • Treat it with the same level of importance as your morning call block. Put it on your calendar. Protect it like your activity numbers.
Task 3: Turn Off Notifications & Share Boundaries
  • Commit to disabling Slack, email, and LinkedIn notifications during personal time.
  • Discuss these rules with your spouse and ask for feedback.
  • If a deal is genuinely urgent, communicate it to your partner upfront: "I need 15 minutes for this—then I'm all yours." Transparency beats silent scrolling.
  • If your manager messages after hours, it can wait until morning. Your call metrics won't suffer from responding at 8 AM instead of 8 PM. Set the boundary.
