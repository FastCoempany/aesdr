Section 01 — The AE Ego Problem
The Root of the Tension: Your Blind Spots
The Root of the Tension: What You're Dealing With

This lesson is going to be uncomfortable. AE ego isn't always intentional — but it is incredibly damaging. From micromanaging SDR outreach to taking credit for meetings you didn't source, these behaviors erode the partnership that feeds your pipeline. The first step is seeing it in yourself.

  • 01Why It Exists: Quota pressure, deal anxiety, and the feeling that you're carrying more weight than your SDR. These are real pressures — but they don't justify the behaviors they produce.
  • 02Impact on Your SDR: When you micromanage, dismiss their qualification, or skip their meetings, they stop bringing you their best work. You get worse leads because you created the conditions for worse leads.
  • 03Impact on Your Results: The AE-SDR relationship is the foundation of your pipeline. When trust breaks down, lead quality drops, handoffs get sloppy, and you're doing twice the work to close half the deals.
The Reality Check
"The SDR who stops going above and beyond for you didn't get lazy. They got tired of being dismissed."
Your ego might be protecting your self-image, but it's actively damaging your pipeline. Every behavior in this lesson has a direct line to your close rate.

If you've worked with an AE who micromanages your outreach, takes credit for your sourced meetings, or skips your syncs — this lesson is for you. AE ego isn't always intentional, but it is incredibly damaging. Understanding the pattern is the first step to navigating it.

  • 01Why It Exists: AEs carry quota pressure, deal anxiety, and often feel like they're shouldering more than their share. These are real pressures — but they produce behaviors that make your job harder.
  • 02Impact on You: You feel disrespected, frustrated, and burnt out. Your best prospecting work gets dismissed or micromanaged. You stop going above and beyond because the effort isn't recognized.
  • 03Impact on Results: When trust breaks down between you and your AE, handoffs get sloppy, feedback stops flowing, and both of you underperform. The ego created the problem; the pipeline pays the price.
The Reality Check
"You can't control your AE's ego. But you can control how you respond to it."
Understanding these patterns gives you the language and strategy to manage up without burning the relationship. That's what this lesson teaches.

Section 02 — Addressing the Ego
Framework for Checking Your Own Ego
Framework for Navigating AE Ego

Step 1 is recognizing the behavior in yourself — not in theory, but in specific interactions from this week. The goal isn't self-flagellation. It's identifying the patterns that damage your pipeline and replacing them with behaviors that build trust.

  • 01Diagnose Your Own Behavior: Are you micromanaging outreach sequences? Skipping SDR syncs? Taking over meetings your SDR sourced? Be honest.
  • 02Ask for Feedback — and Listen: Ask your SDR: "What's one thing I could do differently that would make your job easier?" Then actually do it. Don't defend. Don't explain. Just listen.
  • 03Establish Clear Mutual Expectations: Trust your SDR's qualification. Show up to their meetings. Give credit publicly when they source a great lead. These aren't favors — they're investments in your own pipeline.
The Fix
"The best AEs don't manage SDRs. They partner with them."
Ego feels protective in the moment but costs you in the quarter. Every time you dismiss an SDR's work, you're training them to bring you less of it. Replace ego with trust, and watch the pipeline respond.

Step 1 is naming the behavior — not to assign blame, but to have language for what you're experiencing so you can address it productively. You can't change your AE's ego, but you can change how you navigate it.

  • 01Diagnose the Behavior: Is your AE micromanaging your sequences? Skipping your syncs? Taking credit for your sourced meetings? Put a name on it.
  • 02Use Impact Statements, Not Accusations: "When we skip the weekly sync, I don't know which deals to prioritize in my outreach" hits differently than "You never show up to meetings." Focus on the impact on shared outcomes.
  • 03Propose Solutions, Not Complaints: "Can we do a 15-minute standup Mondays so I know your priority accounts?" is a solution. "You don't communicate" is a dead end. Come with a proposal, not just frustration.
The Fix
"The SDRs who manage AE ego effectively don't fight it — they redirect it."
Your AE's ego isn't going away. But when you show up with data, proposals, and impact statements instead of complaints, you earn the partnership that ego was blocking. That's managing up.

AE Self-Awareness Checklist
  • Do I trust my SDR's qualification, or am I reworking their leads before I even read the notes?
  • Am I showing up to every scheduled SDR sync this week — not just the ones that feel convenient?
  • When my SDR sources a great meeting, do I give them public credit? Or do I just close the deal and move on?
SDR Self-Assessment: Managing Up
  • Can I name the specific AE behavior that's causing friction — without defaulting to "they just don't care"?
  • Have I proposed a solution (standup, shared doc, feedback format) instead of just expressing frustration?
  • Am I documenting my sourced meetings and lead quality so I have data, not just feelings, when the conversation happens?
Actionable Homework
  • Track Key Pain Points—Write down 2–3 examples of AE behaviors that impact your work.
  • Schedule a 1:1—Propose a conversation to share feedback constructively.
  • Self-Assess Your Behaviors—Reflect on how you interact with SDRs and other teams.
  • Commit to One Improvement—Choose one behavior to improve this week (trusting SDR qualification, showing up for meetings, etc.).
