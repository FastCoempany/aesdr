Section 01 — The Circus
Why Sales Survival Needs Rules

The AE-SDR relationship is the engine of your pipeline — and you set the tone. When you skip the huddle, dismiss the qualification, or let ego drive the handoff, the whole machine breaks down. These commandments aren't aspirational. They're operational rules that protect your revenue.

  • 01The Challenge: Your default behaviors under pressure — micromanaging, blaming, going solo — actively damage the partnership that feeds your pipeline.
  • 02The Solution: A shared framework of rules that holds both sides accountable. You can't demand what you don't model.
  • 03The Goal: Build a working relationship where your SDR brings you better leads because they trust you to handle them well.
The Reality Check
"Your SDR's best work goes to the AE who respects it most."
If your SDR has multiple AEs, they will prioritize the one who shows up, gives credit, and closes what they source. Be that AE.

The AE-SDR relationship is critical for your pipeline success, but misalignment leads to chaos. Common problems — ego, lack of communication, blame — fuel the frustration that makes you dread Monday mornings. These commandments give you a framework for what to expect and what to demand.

  • 01The Challenge: Unchecked egos, poor communication, and blame culture make it impossible to do your best prospecting work.
  • 02The Solution: Shared survival rules that give you language for what a healthy AE-SDR relationship looks like — and standing to push back when it doesn't.
  • 03The Goal: Navigate the circus with clarity, self-respect, and a playbook for managing up when the rules get broken.
The Reality Check
"You can't control your AE. But you can control whether you tolerate a broken dynamic."
The SDRs who thrive aren't the ones with perfect AEs. They're the ones who name the problem, propose the fix, and hold the line.

Section 02 — The Ten Commandments
The Essential Rules of Survival

The Ten Commandments hold both sides accountable — but as the senior partner in this relationship, more of them apply directly to you. These aren't suggestions. They're the operating rules that separate AEs who build great SDR partnerships from AEs who burn through them.

  • IDrop the Ego: You're not the hero. You're the closer. Your SDR's work made the opportunity possible. Act like it.
  • IICommunicate Like Adults: Schedule the 10-minute daily huddle. Show up every time. If you cancel, you're telling your SDR their time doesn't matter.
  • IVStop Micromanaging: Trust your SDR's qualification. If it's not where you need it, coach them — don't override them.
  • VIAvoid the Blame Game: When a deal dies, own your part. Run the post-mortem together. Never throw your SDR under the bus in a team meeting.
  • IXDevelop Self-Awareness: If SDRs avoid working with you, that's not their problem. That's your signal to change.
The Stakes
"Your best SDR has other AEs they could prioritize."
Break these rules and your SDR will give their best leads and hardest work to someone else. Pipeline isn't just sourced — it's earned by the AE who deserves it.

The Ten Commandments give you a framework for what a healthy AE-SDR relationship looks like — and the language to push back when it breaks. You have responsibilities too, but you also have the right to expect professionalism, respect, and partnership.

  • IDrop the Ego — Both Ways: Don't expect a parade for one good meeting. But don't accept an AE who takes credit for your sourced deals either.
  • IICommunicate Like Adults: If your AE won't schedule the huddle, propose it yourself. With a calendar invite. And an agenda.
  • IVDemand Autonomy, Earn Trust: You deserve space to run your outreach. But earn that trust by qualifying rigorously and documenting your process.
  • VIRefuse the Blame Game: When a deal dies, don't accept all the blame. But don't dodge your part either. Propose the post-mortem. Be the adult in the room.
  • IXProtect Your Self-Awareness: If you're avoiding feedback or getting defensive, check yourself. Growth requires honesty about where you're falling short too.
The Stakes
"You teach people how to treat you."
If you accept micromanagement, blame, and disrespect without pushing back, you're training your AE that it works. These rules give you the framework to hold the line professionally.

Section 04 — Actionable Homework
Building a Survival Plan This Week
AE/SDR Communication Framework
Meeting TypePurpose & Duration
Daily Huddle10 minutes to align on goals and blockers.
Weekly Check-In30 minutes to share feedback and wins.
Actionable Homework: For AEs
  • Publicly shout out your SDR's contribution in a team meeting this week. Name the specific deal or meeting they sourced.
  • Identify one commandment you've been breaking. Be honest. Write down one specific behavior change you'll implement this week.
  • Schedule (or restart) the daily 10-minute huddle with your SDR. Show up for every one this week. No cancellations.
Actionable Homework: For SDRs
  • Share feedback with your AE about what support you need most. Use an impact statement: "When X happens, it affects Y."
  • Propose one structural fix (daily huddle, shared doc, feedback format) to your AE this week. Come with a calendar invite, not just a complaint.
  • Reflect on one commandment you've been breaking from your side. Growth-mindset SDRs hold themselves accountable too.
