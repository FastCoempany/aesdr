Section 01 — Introduction
The 30% Rule Applies to You Too
The Biggest Threat to Your Career Is You

Most AEs think they're above average. Newsflash: if you're not consistently delivering, you're just another replaceable cog. The same principles that apply to SDRs—coachability, effort, and results—apply to AEs.

  • 01The Reality: If you're not in the top 30% of AEs, you're replaceable.
  • 02The Expectation: Success comes from owning your performance and delivering results.
  • 03The Choice: Top performers create results, middle performers coast, and bottom performers complain. Which one are you?
The Mirror Test
"If you're not self-sourcing, coachable, and driving results, you're at risk."
The 30% rule isn't just a management tool for SDRs. It's the standard you must hold yourself to as an AE.

It's easy to blame the leads, the territory, or your manager. But the hardest question is: are you getting in your own way? The SDRs who make it to AE are the ones who look in the mirror first and fix what they find.

  • 01The Comfort Trap: If you're doing just enough to not get fired, you're already falling behind.
  • 02The Blame Reflex: Every time you blame leads, timing, or territory, you give away control of your own career.
  • 03The Growth Question: Are you actively getting better every week — or just surviving?
The Mirror Test
"If you can't point to specific ways you've improved this month, you're stalling."
The SDR role is a proving ground. The ones who self-reflect, self-correct, and push themselves are the ones who get promoted. Everyone else gets recycled.

Section 02 — The Breakdown
Are You the Problem?
The Habits That Hold You Back

It's easy to point fingers at SDRs when your pipeline is weak. But if your entire strategy relies on SDR-set meetings, that's on you.

  • 01Are You Uncoachable? Uncoachable AEs think they've figured it all out. They say "I know what I'm doing" and blow off pipeline reviews. The top 30% crave feedback, adapt fast, and improve constantly.
  • 02Are You Blaming SDRs? Relying 100% on SDRs for meetings leads to a fragile pipeline. Complaining about lead quality wastes time instead of fixing it.
  • 03Are You Wasting Energy? If you're chasing bad-fit deals to look busy, or avoiding prospecting because it's hard, you're sabotaging your own success.
The Fix
"Top AEs don't wait for success—they create it."
The top AEs source their own meetings, drive their own success, and partner with SDRs—not blame them.

Nobody is going to tell you this in a team meeting, so hear it now: many SDRs sabotage themselves with habits they don't even recognize. Avoiding the phone, cherry-picking leads, venting instead of adjusting — these are career killers disguised as normal frustrations.

  • 01Avoiding the Phone: "I work better over email" is code for "I'm afraid of rejection." The phone is where bookings happen. Stop avoiding it.
  • 02Cherry-Picking Leads: Spending 30 minutes researching each prospect to avoid dialing is procrastination in disguise. Volume creates learning; learning creates quality.
  • 03Venting Instead of Adapting: Complaining about bad leads or bad managers in Slack might feel good, but it signals to everyone that you're in the bottom 30%.
The Fix
"Replace every complaint with an action."
The SDRs who get promoted don't waste energy venting. They take the same bad leads, the same tough territory, and find a way to make it work. That's the difference.

Section 04 — Homework
Audit Yourself as an AE
Break Your Bad Habits This Week
Task 1: Audit Yourself
Self-Reflection AreaKey Questions
CoachabilityWrite down the last 3 pieces of feedback you received. Did you apply them?
Pipeline OwnershipHow many self-sourced meetings did I set in the last month?
FocusList 3 tasks you spent the most time on this week. Were they revenue-driving?
Task 2: Set a Plan to Level Up
  • Identify one area you've been avoiding (prospecting, self-sourcing, or taking feedback).
  • Set a 2-week measurable goal to address it.
  • Track your progress daily.
Task 3: Ask for Real Feedback
  • Schedule a pipeline review with your manager or SDR.
  • Ask: "Where am I underperforming right now?"
  • Ask: "What's one thing I need to change immediately?"
Task 1: Identify Your Self-Sabotage
Bad HabitHonest Check
Phone AvoidanceHow many hours did I spend emailing vs. calling this week? What's my connect rate?
Lead Cherry-PickingHow long do I spend researching before I dial? Am I using "research" to avoid calling?
Complaining vs. AdaptingWrite down the last 3 complaints you made (to peers, in Slack, in your head). What action could replace each one?
Task 2: The 5-Day Challenge
  • For 5 consecutive days, hit 110% of your call target — no excuses, no adjustments.
  • After each day, write one sentence: "Today I improved at ___."
  • At the end of the week, bring your 5 sentences to your manager and ask for feedback on your trajectory.
Task 3: Manage Up With a Purpose
  • Schedule a 15-minute meeting with your manager. Come with: your numbers for the week, one thing you're working to improve, and one question about what top performers do differently.
  • Ask: "What's one habit I should build to get promoted faster?"
  • Follow through on whatever they say — and report back next week.
