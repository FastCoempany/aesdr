Section 01 — The Illusion
Potential Isn't What It Looks Like
Enthusiasm Alone Won't Save You

Managers often get fooled by enthusiastic SDRs who seem promising but fail to deliver. It's easy to see an SDR who's loud, confident, and eager in meetings and think, 'They've got it.' But potential without action is just noise.

  • 01The Enthusiasm Trap: Enthusiasm is not a substitute for competence or results.
  • 02False Hope: "They'll figure it out" is dangerous. If they're not delivering now, don't expect miracles.
  • 03Wasted Energy: Chasing "potential" wastes energy better spent on performers.
The Mindset Shift
"Don't confuse noise with effort."
Results—not personality—should drive your focus. Enthusiasm is great, but it doesn't book meetings.

Being excited about the role is great. But if that's all you bring, you're in danger. Managers have seen dozens of SDRs who light up in team meetings but go dark when it's time to dial. Enthusiasm without execution is the fastest way to lose your manager's trust — and their investment in your growth.

  • 01Energy Isn't Enough: Showing up excited but missing quota is worse than showing up quiet and delivering.
  • 02Actions Speak Loudest: Your manager measures potential by what you produce, not what you promise in 1:1s.
  • 03The Danger Zone: If you're the SDR who "seems great" but can't point to results, your manager is already recategorizing you.
The Mindset Shift
"Don't be the SDR who talks a big game and delivers nothing."
Your manager isn't fooled by enthusiasm. They're measuring bookings, follow-through, and coachability. Make sure the numbers back up the energy.

Section 02 — Red Flags
How to Spot Fake Potential
Are You Faking It Without Knowing It?

A great SDR finds a way to make it work. A mediocre one finds excuses. If the reasons for missed metrics sound polished and rehearsed, you're being played.

  • 01Role-Play Heroes: Training scenarios are controlled and safe. If your SDR sounds polished in role-plays but flops in the wild, they're not delivering where it counts.
  • 02Polished Excuses: "It's a bad time of year" really means "I'm not putting in the effort."
  • 03Avoiding the Grind: "I'm strategizing my calls" usually translates to "I'm procrastinating."
The Fix
"Real potential isn't loud—it's visible in results."
The best SDRs ask smart questions, take action, and hold themselves accountable. They don't need to talk about potential; they prove it.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: you might be faking it and not even realize it. Killing it in role-plays but freezing on live calls. Talking big in team meetings but spending half your day "strategizing" instead of dialing. Your manager sees through all of it.

  • 01Role-Plays Aren't Real: Sounding good in practice doesn't count. Your manager is measuring live calls, real bookings, and actual follow-through.
  • 02Excuses Are Transparent: "The leads are bad" and "It's a slow quarter" are phrases that scream bottom 30%. Replace them with: "Here's what I tried and here's what I'll adjust."
  • 03Strategizing = Stalling: If you're spending more time planning calls than making them, you're hiding from the work.
The Fix
"Prove it with results, not promises."
Stop narrating what you're going to do and start doing it. Your manager doesn't need a strategy deck — they need bookings on the board.

Section 04 — Homework
Audit Your Team for Real Potential
Audit Yourself for Real Potential
Task 1: Audit Your Team
CategoryDescription
Action-TakersConsistent execution, bookings, and ownership.
TalkersHigh enthusiasm, low output, polished excuses.
Task 2: Set Clear Benchmarks
  • Establish performance KPIs: Calls per day, Meetings booked per week, Follow-ups completed.
  • Measure against the KPIs over two weeks and identify who steps up.
Task 3: Commit to Action-Takers
  • Choose 2 SDRs from your top 30%.
  • Create a personalized coaching plan and stretch goals for the next month.
Task 1: Honest Self-Audit
QuestionHonest Answer
Am I hitting my call/email volume targets daily?Write your actual numbers vs. target for the last 5 days.
When I miss a metric, do I explain why or fix it?Write down the last excuse you gave and what you could do instead.
Task 2: Replace Excuses With Actions
  • Write down 3 excuses you've used in the last month (e.g., "bad leads," "slow quarter").
  • Next to each excuse, write the action you'll take instead.
  • Share at least one of these with your manager proactively: "I noticed I was doing X, so I'm changing to Y."
Task 3: Prove It This Week
  • Set a personal stretch goal for this week (e.g., 5 extra calls per day, 2 additional bookings).
  • Track your progress daily and share it with your manager at end of week — without being asked.
